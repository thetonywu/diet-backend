# Is European Food Actually Better Quality Than American Food?

**Categories:** Toxin Avoidance, Animal-Based Diet Basics

## TLDR

Paul Saladino argues that European food is generally higher quality than American food due to stricter regulations on additives, pesticides, and processing. The EU bans or restricts many chemicals permitted in the US food supply — including certain food dyes, preservatives, and glyphosate applications. However, Saladino emphasizes that neither system produces truly optimal food; his recommendation is to source directly from regenerative farms and avoid processed food entirely regardless of country.

## Background

Many Americans notice that food tastes different in Europe, and some report that digestive issues they experience at home disappear when traveling abroad. This has led to widespread interest in whether European food regulations produce meaningfully safer food. Saladino addresses this within his broader framework of toxin avoidance, arguing that the American food supply has been compromised by industrial agriculture, regulatory capture, and the widespread use of chemicals that other developed nations have restricted.

## What Paul Saladino Recommends

Saladino's position is that European food standards are generally superior to American ones, but the real solution is to opt out of the industrial food system entirely.

**Regulatory differences he highlights:**
- The EU bans or restricts over 1,300 chemicals in food and personal care products that are permitted in the United States.
- European countries largely prohibit artificial food dyes (Red 40, Yellow 5, Yellow 6) that are ubiquitous in American processed food. These dyes have been linked to behavioral issues in children.
- The EU applies the precautionary principle — chemicals must be proven safe before approval. The US generally allows chemicals until proven harmful.
- Glyphosate (Roundup) use is far more restricted in the EU. American wheat, oats, and other grains are routinely sprayed with glyphosate as a desiccant before harvest.
- Growth hormones (rBGH/rBST) are banned in EU dairy and meat production but permitted in the US.
- Many European countries do not fluoridate their water supply, while most US municipalities do.

**Specific examples from his content:**
- American cheese products contain less than 50% actual cheese by FDA standards, padded with fillers and seed oils. European cheeses like Parmigiano Reggiano have strict PDO (Protected Designation of Origin) regulations ensuring ingredient purity.
- American bread often contains high-fructose corn syrup, seed oils, and dough conditioners that are banned or uncommon in European bread.
- Conventional American chicken and pork are fed corn and soy diets, concentrating linoleic acid in their fat — a practice less prevalent in traditional European farming.

**His bottom-line recommendation:**
- Don't rely on any government's regulations to protect your health. Source food directly from regenerative farms, eat grass-finished ruminant meat, raw dairy, and organic fruit regardless of which country you live in.
- When traveling in Europe, the food supply is generally safer for dining out — but seed oils are still used in European restaurants.
- Kerrygold (Irish grass-fed butter) is an example of a European product he specifically recommends as superior to most American butter options.

## Key Points

- The EU bans over 1,300 chemicals permitted in US food and personal care products.
- European food labeling is generally more transparent — fewer hidden ingredients and less use of the catch-all term "natural flavors."
- American processed food is significantly more adulterated than European equivalents, but both systems still produce processed food that Saladino recommends avoiding.
- Glyphosate contamination is a major concern in US grain products; Saladino recommends glyphosate-free honey and avoiding all grains regardless.
- European dairy regulations prohibit growth hormones that are standard in US conventional dairy.
- Traditional European cheeses with PDO certification (Parmigiano Reggiano, true mozzarella) are safer choices than American cheese products.
- The quality gap is largest in processed and packaged foods. For whole foods sourced from regenerative farms, the difference between US and European products narrows significantly.

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming all European food is automatically safe.** European processed food still contains seed oils, sugar, and other ingredients Saladino recommends avoiding. The bar is higher, but it's not high enough.
- **Using "European food is better" as justification for eating processed food abroad.** A croissant made with butter in Paris is still a refined grain product.
- **Ignoring sourcing in the US because the system is "broken."** High-quality food exists in America — it just requires going outside the conventional grocery system to regenerative farms and local sources.
- **Thinking organic labels solve the problem.** USDA Organic still permits many practices and inputs that Saladino considers suboptimal. Direct sourcing from known farms is more reliable than any certification.
- **Overlooking water quality.** Many European countries avoid fluoridation, but water quality varies widely. Saladino recommends spring water or properly filtered water regardless of location.

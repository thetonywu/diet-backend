# How to Sleep Better and Fall Asleep Faster

**Categories:** Ancestral Lifestyle, Supplements

## TLDR

Paul Saladino views sleep as a product of circadian rhythm alignment, nutrient status, and environmental hygiene. His protocol centers on morning sunlight exposure to set the circadian clock, adequate carbohydrate intake (fruit and honey) to support serotonin and melatonin production, glycine from connective tissue and bone broth for relaxation, and eliminating blue light and screens 1–2 hours before bed.

## Background

Poor sleep is one of the most common health complaints in the modern world, and Saladino argues it is largely a consequence of living out of alignment with our evolutionary biology. Artificial light, late-night screen use, inadequate sun exposure, low-carb diets, and nutrient deficiencies all disrupt the natural sleep-wake cycle. For people transitioning to the animal-based diet — especially from keto or carnivore — sleep disruption is a common issue, often caused by insufficient carbohydrate intake suppressing serotonin and melatonin production.

## What Paul Saladino Recommends

**Circadian rhythm and light exposure:**
- Get direct sunlight exposure first thing in the morning — this sets the circadian clock and primes melatonin production for the evening.
- Seek sun exposure throughout the day, not just morning. Sunlight drives vitamin D synthesis, nitric oxide production, and hormone regulation.
- Avoid blue light from screens and artificial lighting 1–2 hours before bed. Blue light suppresses melatonin production at night.
- Sleep in a completely dark room.

**Dietary factors for sleep:**
- Eat enough carbohydrates from fruit and honey. Carbs support serotonin production, which converts to melatonin. Going too low-carb is one of the most common causes of poor sleep on animal-based and carnivore diets.
- Eat bone broth, cartilage, and connective tissue regularly. Glycine from these foods serves as a neurotransmitter that promotes relaxation and deep sleep.
- Finish eating 2–3 hours before bed to allow digestion to complete before sleep onset.
- Maintain consistent meal timing to support circadian rhythm.

**Supplements and nutrients:**
- **Magnesium** — specifically magnesium glycinate or threonate (not oxide or citrate). Magnesium supports muscle relaxation, GABA activity, and sleep quality. Take 200–400mg in the evening.

Provides phosphatidylserine, BDNF, DHA, glycine, and B vitamins that support sleep-wake cycles and natural melatonin production.

**Lifestyle practices:**
- Maintain a consistent bedtime schedule.
- Wind down through reading, meditation, or walks — not screens.
- Avoid stimulating content or stressful activities in the evening.
- Exercise regularly (resistance training, outdoor activity), but not too close to bedtime.

## Key Points

- Morning sunlight is the most powerful circadian rhythm signal. It sets the internal clock and primes evening melatonin release.
- Insufficient carbohydrate intake is one of the most common causes of insomnia on animal-based and carnivore diets. Serotonin (the melatonin precursor) requires insulin signaling, which requires carbohydrates.
- Glycine from bone broth, cartilage, and connective tissue directly supports deep, restorative sleep by acting as an inhibitory neurotransmitter.
- Magnesium deficiency is widespread and contributes to poor sleep. Magnesium glycinate or threonate are the preferred forms for sleep support.
- Blue light from screens at night directly suppresses melatonin production via melanopsin receptors in the eyes.
- Eating late at night disrupts both sleep quality and circadian hormone rhythms.
- Consistent sleep and wake times matter more than total hours — regularity trains the circadian system.

## Common Mistakes

- **Going too low-carb.** This is the #1 sleep mistake on animal-based and carnivore diets. Without adequate carbs, serotonin and melatonin production drops, causing insomnia and fragmented sleep.
- **Skipping morning sunlight.** Indoor living with artificial light all day fails to set the circadian clock properly.
- **Using screens until bedtime.** Blue light from phones, tablets, and TVs suppresses melatonin right when you need it most.
- **Taking magnesium oxide or citrate for sleep.** These forms are poorly absorbed and primarily act as laxatives. Use glycinate or threonate instead.
- **Eating large meals right before bed.** Digestion competes with the parasympathetic state needed for deep sleep. Finish eating 2–3 hours before bed.
- **Relying on melatonin supplements.** Saladino's approach is to support the body's own melatonin production through light exposure, carb intake, and nutrient status rather than supplementing with exogenous melatonin.
- **Not eating enough connective tissue.** Muscle meat alone doesn't provide the glycine needed for optimal sleep. Include bone broth, cartilage, or collagen-rich cuts.

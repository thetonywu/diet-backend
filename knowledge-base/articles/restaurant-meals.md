# Are Restaurant Meals Safe? Hidden Dangers When Eating Out

**Categories:** Seed Oils, Toxin Avoidance, Budget & Practical Tips

## TLDR

Paul Saladino warns that virtually all restaurants cook with seed oils, making most restaurant food inflammatory by default. He recommends steakhouses, BBQ joints, and sushi restaurants as the safest options, always asking for butter or tallow instead of vegetable oil, and using resources like localfats.com to find seed-oil-free restaurants.

## Background

One of the most common challenges for anyone following the animal-based diet is eating out. The vast majority of restaurants — from fast food to fine dining — use soybean, canola, or other seed oils for cooking because they are cheap and have high smoke points. Saladino considers seed oils the single most harmful ingredient in the modern food supply, so navigating restaurants requires specific strategies to avoid them.

## What Paul Saladino Recommends

### Safest restaurant types

- **Steakhouses** — most will cook a steak in butter or on a clean grill if you ask; order steak with butter, baked potato, and skip the bread and sauces
- **BBQ restaurants** — smoked meats are generally cooked without seed oils; avoid sauces (most contain soybean oil)
- **Sushi restaurants** — sashimi and nigiri are naturally seed-oil-free; avoid tempura, spicy mayo, and fried rolls
- **Five Guys** — Saladino has specifically approved their burgers, which are cooked on a flat grill without added oils; order bunless with no sauce

### How to order safely

- Always ask: "Can you cook this in butter instead of vegetable oil?"
- Order grilled or broiled meat — never fried
- Skip all sauces and dressings unless you can confirm ingredients (most contain soybean or canola oil)
- Avoid bread, croutons, and anything breaded
- Side of fruit instead of fries when available
- Steak and eggs is the universal safe order at most restaurants

### Restaurants to be cautious about

- **Chipotle** — cooks with rice bran oil (still a seed oil despite marketing)
- **In-N-Out** — fries are cooked in cottonseed oil blend
- **Most Asian restaurants** — heavily reliant on soybean and vegetable oils for wok cooking
- **Italian restaurants** — pasta and bread are grain-based; many sauces use canola oil
- **Fast food chains** — nearly all use seed oils for frying and in sauces

### Finding seed-oil-free restaurants

Saladino recommends **localfats.com**, a community-built directory of restaurants that cook with animal fats or non-seed-oil alternatives. The site allows you to search by location and find restaurants committed to using butter, tallow, or other traditional fats.

## Key Points

- Seed oils are the default cooking fat in almost every restaurant — the burden is on you to ask
- A single restaurant meal cooked in seed oil can deliver more linoleic acid than an entire day of home-cooked animal-based food
- Steakhouses and BBQ joints are the safest choices because their primary cooking methods (grilling, smoking) don't require seed oils
- Sashimi and nigiri sushi are naturally safe; avoid anything fried or sauced
- Even "healthy" restaurants (Mediterranean, farm-to-table) often cook with canola or grapeseed oil
- localfats.com is the best resource for finding restaurants that avoid seed oils

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming expensive restaurants use better oils** — most fine dining kitchens still use canola or blended vegetable oils
- **Trusting "cooked in olive oil" claims** — many restaurants cut olive oil with cheaper seed oils, or use "olive oil blend" that is mostly canola
- **Ordering salads thinking they are safe** — dressings are almost always seed-oil-based
- **Forgetting about hidden sources** — bread, croutons, marinades, and even seasoning blends can contain seed oils
- **Not asking** — simply asking the kitchen to use butter instead of oil works at most restaurants

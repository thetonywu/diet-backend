# How Do I Eat Animal-Based on a Budget?

**Categories:** Budget & Practical Tips, Animal-Based Diet Basics

## TLDR

Eating animal-based costs roughly $12–$18 per day ($400–$600/month) when you prioritize affordable cuts like beef shanks, ground beef, and organ meats over premium steaks. Organ meats are among the cheapest and most nutrient-dense foods available — butchers often sell them for around $5/lb or give them away. Buying grass-fed suet for cooking fat and sourcing from regenerative farms online can further reduce costs.

## Background

One of the most common objections to the animal-based diet is cost. People assume that eating grass-fed meat and organs every day is inherently expensive, especially compared to a grain-heavy standard diet. In reality, the perceived expense comes from defaulting to premium cuts like ribeyes and filets. Saladino and his team argue that with strategic sourcing and a willingness to eat less popular cuts, animal-based eating can fit within a typical grocery budget.

## What Paul Saladino Recommends

Saladino's framework for affordable animal-based eating centers on choosing the right cuts and sourcing strategically.

**Budget-friendly daily meal plan (for a 150 lb person):**
- 1.5 lbs grass-fed beef shank at ~$5.50/lb = ~$7.75
- 150g grass-fed beef suet at ~$6.00/lb = ~$2.16
- 3 oz grass-fed beef liver at ~$10.99/lb = ~$2.06
- **Daily total: ~$12**

**Alternative with ground beef:**
- 1.5 lbs grass-fed ground beef at ~$8.49/lb = ~$12.69
- Same suet and liver = ~$16.91/day (~$500/month)

**Actionable cost-cutting strategies:**
- Skip expensive ribeyes ($15–$20/lb) and buy ground beef, stew meat, skirt steak, chuck steak, bavette, and sirloin ($7–$10/lb for grass-finished).
- Buy organ meats directly from butchers — liver, heart, and kidney are often sold for ~$5/lb or sometimes given away free since demand is low.
- Use grass-finished suet or beef trimmings (~$5/lb max) as your primary cooking fat instead of expensive tallow products.
- Add seasonal fruit, raw honey, and raw milk as your carb sources — these are relatively affordable per calorie.
- If organ meats aren't accessible or palatable, Heart & Soil desiccated organ supplements can fill the gap.

**Recommended online sources for affordable grass-fed meat:**
- White Oak Pastures (whiteoakpastures.com) — regenerative farm in Georgia with competitive pricing on shanks, ground beef, suet, and liver.
- Force of Nature Meats (forceofnature.com) — Texas-based regenerative operation specializing in affordable ground meat, plus venison, elk, and bison.

## Key Points

- A full day of animal-based eating can cost as little as $12 when built around shanks, suet, and liver.
- Organ meats are the cheapest superfoods available — often $5/lb or less from local butchers.
- Grass-fed ground beef is the most practical everyday staple, running $7–$10/lb from regenerative sources.
- Grass-fed suet provides cooking fat at a fraction of the cost of rendered tallow products.
- The real comparison isn't animal-based vs. rice and beans — it's animal-based vs. what most Americans actually spend on food, including restaurants, coffee, and processed snacks.
- If budget is extremely tight, buying conventional lean cuts and supplementing with grass-fed fat is a reasonable compromise. Avoid conventional animal fat (high in omega-6 from grain feeding) but lean conventional beef is acceptable.
- Buying in bulk, using subscribe-and-save programs, and purchasing whole or half animals from local farms can reduce per-pound costs significantly.

## Common Mistakes

- **Defaulting to premium steaks.** Ribeyes and filet mignon are unnecessary. Ground beef, shanks, and chuck are just as nutritious.
- **Ignoring organ meats.** They are the most nutrient-dense and often the cheapest cuts at the butcher counter.
- **Buying rendered tallow products at specialty prices.** Raw suet from a butcher is far cheaper and can be rendered at home.
- **Cutting corners with conventional chicken and pork.** These are cheap but high in omega-6 from corn and soy feeding. Conventional lean beef is a better budget compromise.
- **Not accounting for what you're no longer buying.** Eliminating processed foods, snacks, dining out, and supplements you no longer need offsets much of the meat cost.
- **Thinking you need all grass-fed or nothing.** If budget is tight, prioritize grass-fed fat and organs while using conventional lean beef as a bridge.

# What's the Difference Between Animal-Based and Carnivore?

**Categories:** Animal-Based Diet Basics

## TLDR

The carnivore diet excludes all plant foods, restricting eating to meat, fish, eggs, and sometimes dairy. Paul Saladino's animal-based diet keeps animal foods as the foundation but adds fruit, honey, and raw dairy as nutrient-dense, low-toxin complements. Saladino himself transitioned away from strict carnivore after experiencing hormonal and sleep issues on long-term zero-carb eating.

## Background

Both diets prioritize animal foods and reject industrially processed foods, grains, legumes, and most vegetables. The distinction matters because many people encounter carnivore first, then wonder whether they need carbohydrates at all — and whether fruit and honey are acceptable. Paul Saladino spent years as a prominent carnivore advocate before publicly revising his position, making him a central figure in this debate.

## What Paul Saladino Recommends

Saladino practiced strict carnivore from roughly 2018 to 2020. After experiencing sleep disturbances, heart palpitations, muscle cramps, and a drop in testosterone levels, he concluded that long-term ketosis was not optimal for him — or, he argues, for most people. He added fruit and honey back in and coined the term "animal-based" to describe the resulting framework.

His current position on the two diets:

- **Carnivore is a useful elimination protocol**, but not necessarily an ideal long-term diet for everyone. It can help identify food sensitivities and reduce plant-toxin exposure, but strict zero-carb eating may impair sleep, hormones, and athletic performance over time.
- **Animal-based keeps the best of carnivore** — nose-to-tail animal foods, organ meats, no seed oils, no processed grains — while adding back foods he considers low-toxin and nutrient-dense: ripe fruit, raw honey, and raw dairy.
- **Fruit and honey are the preferred carbohydrate sources** because they carry far fewer defensive plant chemicals ("toxins") than seeds, grains, legumes, or most vegetables. Ripe fruit in particular is designed by nature to be eaten, so it has minimal anti-nutrients.
- **Carbohydrate targets scale with activity level**: roughly 0.7g–1.7g per pound of body weight depending on exercise volume, sourced primarily from fruit and honey.
- **Organ meats remain non-negotiable**: at least 0.5 oz of liver daily (2–3 oz per week), plus heart, kidney, and bone marrow for micronutrient density that muscle meat alone cannot provide.
- **Raw dairy is preferred over pasteurized** because heat processing degrades enzymes and certain nutrients. Raw milk, kefir, and cheese are considered suitable additions for those who tolerate dairy.
- **Vegetables are still largely avoided** even on animal-based — particularly seeds, nuts, legumes, and leafy greens high in oxalates. Some low-toxin options like squash, zucchini, and cucumber are accepted by some practitioners.

## Key Points

- Carnivore = animal foods only; zero plant foods including fruit. Animal-based = animal foods as the foundation, with fruit, honey, and raw dairy added.
- Paul Saladino publicly left strict carnivore after experiencing hormonal decline and sleep disruption on zero-carb eating.
- His reasoning for including fruit: it contains the fewest plant defense chemicals of any plant category. Seeds, grains, and legumes carry the highest toxin burden; ripe fruit is the lowest.
- Both diets eliminate seed oils, processed grains, legumes, and refined sugar.
- Animal-based is not a plant-based diet with meat added — animal foods (especially organs) remain the nutritional centerpiece.
- Honey is considered animal-based because it is produced by animals (bees), making it acceptable even by stricter interpretations of the framework.
- People who do well long-term on carnivore often have specific goals (autoimmune elimination, severe gut issues) where eliminating all plants provides a cleaner baseline.
- Saladino now consumes approximately 300 grams of carbohydrates per day, primarily from fruit and honey — a dramatic departure from zero-carb carnivore.

## Common Mistakes

- Assuming animal-based and carnivore are interchangeable terms — they are not. Carnivore is zero-plant; animal-based allows fruit, honey, and raw dairy.
- Treating the animal-based diet as a high-vegetable diet that happens to include meat. Vegetables (especially seeds and grains) remain mostly excluded.
- Skipping organ meats. Both carnivore and animal-based practitioners emphasize organs as critical for micronutrient completeness; muscle meat alone is insufficient.
- Choosing pasteurized dairy when raw is the preferred form on animal-based.
- Adding carbohydrates from grains or legumes instead of fruit and honey. The plant-toxin logic is central to which carbs are acceptable.
- Thinking carnivore is always the more advanced or healthier option. Saladino's position is that long-term zero-carb may undermine hormones and sleep for many people.
- Using dried fruit or fruit juice — Saladino recommends whole, ripe, fresh fruit rather than concentrated or processed fruit products.

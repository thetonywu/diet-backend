# How to Reverse Insulin Resistance on the Animal-Based Diet

**Categories:** Chronic Disease, Seed Oils, Nutrients

## TLDR

Paul Saladino argues that seed oils and linoleic acid — not carbohydrates — are the primary driver of insulin resistance. His protocol centers on eliminating seed oils and processed foods, eating grass-fed ruminant meat with organs, and including carbohydrates from fruit and honey (90–120g/day for those actively correcting metabolic dysfunction, scaling up as health improves). He tracks fasting insulin as his primary metabolic marker, considering anything above ~5 μIU/mL a red flag.

## Background

Insulin resistance is the hallmark of metabolic syndrome and underlies type 2 diabetes, obesity, cardiovascular disease, and NAFLD. The conventional approach blames excess carbohydrate intake and prescribes low-carb or ketogenic diets. Saladino challenges this directly: he argues the root cause is excess linoleic acid from seed oils, which damages mitochondria and fat cells, and that healthy carbohydrates from fruit and honey are not only safe but beneficial for metabolic recovery.

## What Paul Saladino Recommends

### Root cause: seed oils, not carbs

Saladino's central thesis is that excess linoleic acid (omega-6 fat) from seed oils breaks the metabolic machinery:

- Linoleic acid accumulates in fat cells and oxidizes into harmful byproducts (OXLAMs) including 4-HNE and 13-HODE
- These oxidized metabolites cause mitochondrial dysfunction — the signature of metabolic disease
- Dysfunctional, enlarged fat cells release free fatty acids that induce insulin resistance in peripheral muscles
- Ancestral populations consumed ~2–3% of calories from linoleic acid; modern Americans consume up to 20%
- Hidden sources beyond obvious cooking oils: nuts, seeds, conventional chicken, and pork (which store linoleic acid from corn/soy feed)

### Dietary protocol

**Step 1: Eliminate the root cause**
- Remove all seed oils: canola, soybean, corn, sunflower, safflower, cottonseed, grapeseed, rice bran
- Remove all processed foods — bread, pasta, cereals, granola, crackers, sauces
- Cook with tallow, butter, ghee, or coconut oil

**Step 2: Eat nutrient-dense animal foods**
- Grass-fed, grass-finished ruminant meat (beef, lamb, bison) — 1g protein per pound of goal body weight
- Organ meats weekly (liver, heart, kidney)
- Pasture-raised eggs, wild-caught fish in moderation

**Step 3: Include species-appropriate carbohydrates**
- For active metabolic dysfunction: start at 90–120g carbs/day from fruit and honey
- As metabolic health improves, scale up to 0.7–1.2g per pound of body weight
- Sources: seasonal fruit, raw honey, raw dairy (if tolerated)
- A 30-day strict carnivore/keto phase may be useful as a strategic elimination protocol, but long-term ketosis is not the goal

### Blood markers to track

**Primary metabolic markers:**
- **Fasting insulin** — Saladino's top recommendation; he considers >5 μIU/mL problematic, even when fasting glucose appears normal
- **Triglyceride-to-HDL ratio** — target below 1.5; a proxy for insulin resistance when fasting insulin isn't available
- **Hemoglobin A1c** — long-term blood sugar control
- **hsCRP** — systemic inflammation marker
- **Liver enzymes and GGT** — for NAFLD assessment
- **Uric acid** — metabolic health indicator

**His view on standard reference ranges:** the conventional fasting insulin upper limit of ~20 μIU/mL is dangerously loose; metabolic dysfunction often begins well before glucose abnormalities appear on standard labs.

## Key Points

- Saladino explicitly rejects the claim that carbohydrates cause insulin resistance — his answer is seed oils and linoleic acid
- He distinguishes whole-food sugars (fruit, honey) from processed sugars (HFCS, refined sugar); he cites research suggesting honey may actually improve insulin sensitivity
- The omega-6:omega-3 ratio has shifted from an ancestral ~1:1 to as high as 20:1 in the modern diet, primarily due to seed oil consumption
- Linoleic acid takes years to fully clear from fat tissue — expect gradual improvement over months, not weeks
- Many "keto" products ironically contain seed oils, meaning people may be restricting carbs while still consuming the actual driver of their insulin resistance
- He cites a study showing dietary fat (not fructose) stimulates NAFLD development more potently in animal models
- Fasting insulin is a more sensitive early marker of metabolic dysfunction than fasting glucose or A1c

## Common Mistakes

- **Blaming carbohydrates and going strict keto long-term** — Saladino views long-term ketosis as a hormonal stressor that raises cortisol and SHBG while missing the real culprit (seed oils)
- **Eating "keto" processed foods** — many keto bars, snacks, and dressings contain seed oils, defeating the purpose
- **Substituting chicken for beef as a "healthier" protein** — conventional chicken has very high linoleic acid content in its fat
- **Ignoring fasting insulin because fasting glucose is normal** — insulin resistance can be present for years before glucose dysregulation appears on labs
- **Expecting immediate results** — because linoleic acid incorporates into cell membranes and fat tissue, full metabolic recovery takes months to years of consistent dietary change
- **Avoiding fruit out of sugar fear** — whole-food fructose from fruit behaves differently than HFCS; Saladino cites evidence that it does not alter gut permeability or microbiota in healthy humans ([PMC8358846](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8358846/))

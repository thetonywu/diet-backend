# How Do I Support a Healthy Pregnancy on the Animal-Based Diet?

**Categories:** Women's Health, Fertility, Nutrients

## TLDR

Saladino positions the animal-based diet as the optimal prenatal nutrition strategy, with liver as "nature's prenatal vitamin." He emphasizes bioavailable folate, retinol, choline, DHA, and heme iron from animal foods rather than synthetic prenatal supplements. Adequate carbohydrates from fruit and honey (150–250g/day) are critical to support thyroid function and progesterone production during pregnancy.

## Background

Conventional prenatal advice centers on synthetic folic acid supplements, limiting fish intake, and avoiding raw dairy and undercooked eggs. Saladino challenges much of this framework, arguing that the nutrients needed for healthy fetal development — folate, retinol, choline, DHA, iron — are far more bioavailable from animal foods than from supplements or plants. He considers the standard prenatal vitamin a poor substitute for nose-to-tail eating and warns that many women enter pregnancy already depleted from years of seed oil consumption and nutrient-poor diets.

## What Paul Saladino Recommends

**The foundation: liver as the prenatal vitamin**

Saladino calls liver "nature's prenatal vitamin" because it provides the exact nutrients needed for fetal development in their most bioavailable forms:
- **Methylfolate** — the active form of folate, critical for neural tube development. Unlike synthetic folic acid in prenatal vitamins, methylfolate from liver does not require MTHFR enzyme conversion. Many women have MTHFR polymorphisms that impair folic acid conversion, making liver a safer and more reliable folate source.
- **Retinol (vitamin A)** — the preformed, bioavailable version. Plant sources provide only beta-carotene, which converts poorly. Retinol is critical for fetal organ development.
- **Choline** — essential for fetal brain development. Most prenatal vitamins don't contain adequate choline.
- **B12** — critical for neurological development.
- **Heme iron** — supports the 50% blood volume expansion during pregnancy. Far better absorbed than plant-based non-heme iron.

Recommended dose: 0.5 oz liver per day or 2–3 oz per week. Don't overdo it — excessive vitamin A from liver can be harmful during pregnancy.

**Macronutrient guidance:**
- **Carbohydrates:** 150–250g per day from fruit and honey. This is critical — very low-carb diets suppress thyroid function and can impair progesterone production, both of which are essential for maintaining pregnancy.
- **Protein:** ~1 lb grass-fed ruminant meat per 100 lbs body weight daily.
- **Fat:** Primarily from animal sources — tallow, butter, ghee, and the fat in grass-fed meat.

**Priority foods during pregnancy:**
- Grass-fed ruminant meat (beef, bison, lamb) — zinc, iron, B vitamins, complete protein
- Liver (0.5 oz/day) — folate, retinol, choline, B12, iron
- Other organs (heart, kidney) — CoQ10, selenium, B vitamins
- Pasture-raised eggs — choline, fat-soluble vitamins (cook whites, yolks can be soft)
- Raw dairy (raw kefir, raw cheese, raw butter) — fat-soluble vitamins A, D, K2, calcium, probiotics
- Bone broth — glycine, collagen, minerals
- Ripe low-toxin fruit (berries, watermelon, papaya, citrus) — vitamin C, carbohydrates
- Raw honey — carbohydrate source with enzymes and minerals
- Wild-caught small fish (sardines, salmon) — DHA, EPA, selenium. Limit to twice a week and choose small species to minimize mercury.

**Foods and substances to avoid:**
- All seed oils — inflammatory, endocrine-disrupting
- Processed foods and refined carbohydrates
- Most vegetables — especially nightshades (lectins), leafy greens (oxalates), and cruciferous vegetables (goitrogens that impair thyroid)
- Conventional chicken and pork — high omega-6 from corn/soy feeding
- Large predatory fish (tuna, swordfish) — mercury bioaccumulation
- Farmed fish — heavy metals, microplastics, antibiotics
- Alcohol
- Environmental endocrine disruptors — plastics (BPA), non-stick cookware (PFAS), chemical sunscreens, synthetic fragrances

**On conventional prenatal advice:**
- Saladino disagrees with the standard recommendation to take synthetic folic acid — liver provides methylfolate directly, bypassing MTHFR conversion issues
- He recommends wild-caught fish for DHA rather than fish oil supplements, which are typically oxidized
- His framework promotes raw dairy, which contradicts conventional pregnancy advice to avoid unpasteurized products due to Listeria risk — this is an area where his views diverge from mainstream obstetric guidance

## Key Points

- Liver is "nature's prenatal vitamin" — provides methylfolate, retinol, choline, B12, and heme iron in bioavailable forms
- Synthetic folic acid requires MTHFR enzyme conversion that many women cannot perform efficiently; liver provides active methylfolate directly
- Adequate carbohydrates (150–250g/day from fruit and honey) are critical during pregnancy to support thyroid and progesterone
- Very low-carb diets can suppress thyroid function and impair fertility and pregnancy maintenance
- Vitamin A from liver is essential for fetal development, but don't exceed 0.5 oz liver/day to avoid excess
- Choline from liver and egg yolks supports fetal brain development — most prenatal vitamins are deficient in choline
- DHA from wild-caught fish and grass-fed beef fat supports fetal brain and neural development
- Heme iron from red meat and liver is far better absorbed than supplemental or plant-based iron
- Seed oils and environmental endocrine disruptors (plastics, PFAS, synthetic chemicals) should be aggressively eliminated during pregnancy
- Heart & Soil Her Package is the dedicated female reproductive supplement

## Common Mistakes

- **Taking synthetic folic acid instead of eating liver** — many women have MTHFR polymorphisms that impair conversion of folic acid to active methylfolate; liver provides the active form directly
- **Going very low-carb during pregnancy** — this can suppress thyroid function and cortisol regulation, impairing progesterone production needed to maintain pregnancy
- **Relying on prenatal vitamins as nutritional insurance** — synthetic multivitamins have poor bioavailability and industrial fillers; they're not a substitute for nutrient-dense animal foods
- **Avoiding red meat during pregnancy** — red meat provides heme iron, zinc, and B vitamins in forms far more bioavailable than plant sources or supplements
- **Taking fish oil for DHA** — most commercial fish oil is oxidized; eat wild-caught fatty fish instead
- **Eating large predatory fish frequently** — mercury accumulates up the food chain; choose sardines, salmon, and other small species, and limit to twice a week
- **Not eating enough** — pregnancy requires increased caloric intake; restricting food or fasting during pregnancy is counterproductive

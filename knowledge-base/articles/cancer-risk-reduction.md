# How Do I Reduce Cancer Risk on the Animal-Based Diet?

**Categories:** Chronic Disease, Toxin Avoidance

## TLDR

Paul Saladino positions cancer risk reduction within his broader metabolic health framework: fix insulin resistance, eliminate seed oils, remove environmental carcinogens (PFAS, plastics, phthalates), and provide the body with nutrient-dense animal foods to support cellular repair and immune function. He challenges the mainstream narrative that red meat causes cancer, arguing that metabolic dysfunction and environmental toxin exposure are the actual drivers, while nutrient-dense animal foods provide the raw materials for DNA repair and immune surveillance.

## Background

Cancer is frequently cited as a reason to avoid red meat, with organizations like the WHO classifying processed meat as a Group 1 carcinogen and red meat as Group 2A ("probably carcinogenic"). Saladino disputes this classification, arguing that the observational studies behind it fail to separate red meat consumption from the broader context of the standard American diet — which includes seed oils, processed food, and environmental toxins. His framework treats cancer risk as a downstream consequence of the same metabolic dysfunction that drives other chronic diseases.

## What Paul Saladino Recommends

**His position on red meat and cancer:**
- The epidemiological studies linking red meat to cancer are observational and confounded by the "healthy user bias" — people who eat less red meat in Western populations also tend to exercise more, smoke less, and follow other health-conscious behaviors.
- Processed meat (hot dogs, deli meats, bacon with nitrites and seed oils) is a fundamentally different product from grass-fed steak. Lumping them together is misleading.
- Ancestral populations consuming primarily animal foods did not exhibit the cancer rates seen in modern industrialized societies.
- Grass-fed ruminant meat provides nutrients that support anti-cancer mechanisms: selenium (antioxidant defense), zinc (immune function), retinol (cellular differentiation), and CLA (conjugated linoleic acid, which has shown anti-tumor properties in animal studies).

**Metabolic health as cancer prevention:**
- Insulin resistance creates a pro-growth, pro-inflammatory environment. Elevated insulin and IGF-1 in metabolically unhealthy people promote cell proliferation.
- Seed oils damage mitochondria through linoleic acid oxidation. Dysfunctional mitochondria shift cellular energy production toward glycolysis (the Warburg effect), which is characteristic of cancer cells.
- Eliminating seed oils and restoring insulin sensitivity (fasting insulin < 5 μIU/mL) addresses the metabolic terrain in which cancer develops.

**Environmental carcinogen avoidance:**
- PFAS ("forever chemicals") from non-stick cookware — linked to hormonal disruption and cancer. Replace with cast iron or stainless steel.
- Plastics (BPA, BPS, BPE) — endocrine disruptors that may promote hormone-sensitive cancers. Replace all plastic food storage with glass.
- 1,4-dioxane in conventional laundry detergent — classified as a probable carcinogen. Use white vinegar instead.
- Glyphosate — classified as "probably carcinogenic" by the WHO's IARC. Present in conventional grains and some honey. Choose glyphosate-free honey and avoid all grains.
- Chemical sunscreens (oxybenzone, avobenzone, octinoxate) — absorbed systemically with potential carcinogenic and endocrine-disrupting properties. Use zinc oxide mineral sunscreen only when needed.
- EMFs — classified as "possibly carcinogenic" (Group 2B) by WHO's IARC. Minimize exposure through wired connections and phone hygiene.

**Nutrient-dense foods for cellular protection:**
- Liver — retinol (vitamin A) supports cellular differentiation, the process by which cells mature normally rather than proliferating abnormally. Also provides folate for DNA repair.
- Heart — CoQ10 supports mitochondrial function and energy production, counteracting the mitochondrial dysfunction associated with cancer development.
- Creatine monohydrate (5g/day) — frees ~50% of daily methylation capacity, which is required for DNA repair, gene expression regulation, and immune function.
- Colostrum — contains immune-modulating growth factors and supports gut barrier integrity, preventing chronic systemic inflammation.
- Selenium from kidney — supports antioxidant defense via glutathione peroxidase.

**Sun exposure (controlled):**
- Saladino argues that ancestral populations had extensive sun exposure without epidemic skin cancer rates.
- He attributes modern skin cancer susceptibility partly to seed oil consumption — linoleic acid in skin tissue increases oxidative damage from UV radiation.
- Eliminating seed oils from the diet may reduce sunburn susceptibility, changing the risk-benefit calculation for sun exposure.
- Vitamin D, synthesized from UVB exposure, plays a role in immune surveillance and has been associated with lower cancer risk in observational studies.

## Key Points

- Saladino treats cancer risk reduction as part of the same metabolic health framework that addresses heart disease, diabetes, and autoimmune conditions — not as a separate intervention.
- Insulin resistance creates a metabolic environment that favors cancer cell proliferation. Restoring insulin sensitivity through diet is the primary prevention strategy.
- Seed oil-driven mitochondrial dysfunction may promote the shift to glycolysis (Warburg effect) that characterizes cancer cells.
- The red meat-cancer link from observational studies is confounded by the standard American diet and fails to distinguish grass-fed steak from processed deli meat.
- Environmental carcinogens (PFAS, plastics, glyphosate, chemical sunscreens) are significant and underappreciated risk factors that the animal-based lifestyle protocol addresses through systematic toxin elimination.
- Creatine supplementation frees methylation capacity for DNA repair — a direct mechanism for supporting anti-cancer cellular maintenance.
- Liver provides retinol, which supports normal cellular differentiation — the process that goes awry in cancer development.
- This represents Saladino's perspective within the ancestral health community. Mainstream oncology does not endorse these views as cancer prevention, and individuals with cancer or elevated cancer risk should work with their oncology team.

## Common Mistakes

- **Avoiding red meat because of cancer fears.** The observational data does not distinguish between grass-fed steak and processed meat consumed alongside seed oils, refined sugar, and other standard American diet components.
- **Ignoring environmental carcinogen exposure while focusing on diet.** PFAS from cookware, plastics in food storage, and glyphosate in grains represent significant ongoing exposure that diet alone does not address.
- **Using chemical sunscreen daily "to prevent skin cancer."** Chemical sunscreens are absorbed systemically and contain endocrine-disrupting ingredients. Zinc oxide mineral sunscreen is the safer option when sun protection is needed.
- **Taking fish oil for its anti-inflammatory "cancer-protective" properties.** Most commercial fish oil is oxidized and may be pro-inflammatory.
- **Assuming cancer prevention requires specialized supplements or protocols.** In Saladino's framework, the same animal-based diet and toxin avoidance protocol that addresses all chronic disease also addresses cancer risk.
- **Conflating processed meat with whole-animal foods.** Hot dogs, deli meats, and conventional bacon (with nitrites, seed oils, and preservatives) are not equivalent to grass-fed steak and organ meats.

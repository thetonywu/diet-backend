# How Do I Optimize Postpartum Recovery?

**Categories:** Women's Health, Fertility, Nutrients

## TLDR

Paul Saladino recommends continuing the same nutrient-dense animal-based protocol used during pregnancy into the postpartum period, with emphasis on liver for retinol and methylfolate, bone broth for collagen and glycine, adequate carbohydrates (150–250g/day) to support thyroid function and milk production, and raw dairy for calcium and fat-soluble vitamins. Recovery and breastfeeding dramatically increase nutrient demands — organ meats and the animal-based diet are positioned as the most efficient way to meet them.

## Background

Postpartum recovery is one of the most nutrient-demanding periods in a woman's life. The body is simultaneously healing from childbirth, producing breast milk, managing significant hormonal shifts (progesterone and estrogen drop sharply after delivery), and coping with severe sleep disruption. Saladino's framework treats postpartum nutrition as an extension of his pregnancy protocol — the same foods that support fetal development also support maternal recovery and lactation. He emphasizes that nutrient-dense animal foods provide the bioavailable forms of vitamins and minerals that synthetic postnatal supplements attempt to replicate.

## What Paul Saladino Recommends

**Core postpartum nutrition (same foundation as pregnancy):**
- Grass-fed ruminant meat: at least 1 lb per day for protein, iron, zinc, and B12
- Liver: 0.5 oz per day maximum for retinol (vitamin A), methylfolate, choline, heme iron, and B12. Saladino calls liver "nature's prenatal vitamin" and its importance extends into postpartum. Vitamin A is specifically noted as "critical during pregnancy and breastfeeding."
- Bone broth: daily for glycine, collagen, and connective tissue support. Glycine supports tissue repair, gut healing, and sleep quality — all crucial during postpartum recovery.
- Pasture-raised eggs: daily for choline (critical for infant brain development via breast milk) and fat-soluble vitamins.
- Raw dairy: raw milk and raw cheese for calcium, vitamin K2, and bioavailable protein. Calcium demands increase significantly during breastfeeding.
- Wild-caught small fish (sardines, salmon, mackerel): for DHA and EPA, which transfer to breast milk and support infant neurological development. Avoid large predatory fish (tuna, swordfish) due to mercury.

**Carbohydrates:**
- 150–250g/day from fruit and honey. Adequate carbohydrates are critical to support thyroid function and progesterone production. Very low-carb diets suppress thyroid conversion of T4 to T3, which can worsen postpartum fatigue and impair milk supply.
- Preferred sources: ripe bananas, mangoes, berries, watermelon, oranges, raw honey, fresh-squeezed orange juice.

**Key nutrients for breastfeeding:**
- Retinol (liver): transfers to breast milk and supports infant immune development
- Choline (eggs, liver): critical for infant brain development
- DHA (small fatty fish, salmon roe, beef brain): supports infant neurological development
- Folate as methylfolate (liver): superior to synthetic folic acid, especially for women with MTHFR polymorphisms
- Iron as heme iron (red meat, liver): replenishes stores depleted during pregnancy and delivery
- Calcium (raw dairy, bone broth): demands increase significantly during lactation

**Supplements:**
- Heart & Soil Her Package (ovary, uterus, fallopian tube, liver, kidney): for women's hormonal recovery
- Heart & Soil Beef Organs: 6 capsules/day if not eating fresh organs — provides liver, heart, kidney, pancreas, spleen
- Heart & Soil Firestarter (thyroid and adrenal tissue): for thyroid support, especially if experiencing postpartum fatigue or sluggish thyroid
- Heart & Soil Colostrum: for gut healing and immune support
- Magnesium glycinate or threonate: 200–400mg/day for sleep quality and muscle recovery
- Vitamin D3 only if sun exposure is limited

**Lifestyle:**
- Morning sunlight as soon as possible after waking — helps reset circadian rhythm disrupted by nighttime feedings
- Sleep whenever the baby sleeps. Glycine from bone broth and magnesium before bed support deeper sleep during limited sleep windows.
- Gentle movement and walking — not intense exercise in the early postpartum weeks. Reintroduce resistance training gradually.
- Continue toxin avoidance: avoid plastics, non-stick cookware, and conventional personal care products. Endocrine disruptors can transfer to breast milk.

## Key Points

- Postpartum nutrient demands exceed even pregnancy demands due to simultaneous recovery and milk production.
- Liver is the single most important food for postpartum recovery — it provides the bioavailable forms of retinol, folate, iron, and B12 that synthetic postnatal vitamins attempt to replicate.
- Vitamin A (retinol from liver) is specifically critical during breastfeeding — it transfers to breast milk and supports infant immune function.
- Adequate carbohydrates (150–250g/day) support thyroid function and milk supply. Going too low-carb postpartum can worsen fatigue and suppress lactation.
- Bone broth provides glycine for tissue repair, gut healing, and sleep support — all essential during postpartum recovery.
- DHA from small fatty fish and salmon roe transfers to breast milk and supports infant brain development. Fish oil supplements are not recommended due to oxidation concerns.
- Environmental toxins (plastics, PFAS, phthalates) can transfer to breast milk, making toxin avoidance especially important during nursing.
- The animal-based diet framework positions whole foods as superior to synthetic postnatal supplements, which use less bioavailable nutrient forms and contain industrial fillers.

## Common Mistakes

- **Stopping organ meats after delivery.** Postpartum and breastfeeding are when nutrient demands are highest. Continue liver, bone broth, and organ meats throughout nursing.
- **Restricting carbohydrates postpartum.** Low-carb diets suppress thyroid function and can impair milk production. This is not the time for keto or carnivore.
- **Relying on synthetic postnatal vitamins instead of food.** Synthetic folic acid, ferrous sulfate, and retinyl palmitate are less bioavailable than their food-sourced equivalents from liver and red meat.
- **Neglecting hydration and electrolytes.** Breastfeeding increases fluid demands significantly. Raw milk, bone broth, coconut water, and water with sea salt all contribute.
- **Jumping back into intense exercise too soon.** Postpartum recovery requires gradual reintroduction of movement. Walking first, then resistance training as the body heals.
- **Ignoring thyroid function.** Postpartum thyroiditis affects up to 10% of women. Adequate carbs, selenium from kidney, and avoiding goitrogens (cruciferous vegetables, fluoridated water) support thyroid recovery.
- **Eating large predatory fish for DHA.** Mercury accumulates in tuna and swordfish and transfers to breast milk. Choose sardines, salmon, and mackerel instead.

# Is Dairy Okay? What's Wrong With Conventional Milk?

**Categories:** Animal-Based Diet Basics, Nutrients

## TLDR

Dairy is a core pillar of the animal-based diet — but only raw dairy. Saladino calls conventional pasteurized milk "dead milk" because heating destroys the enzymes, beneficial bacteria, and immune proteins that make dairy nutritious. Raw milk, raw cheese, raw butter, and raw kefir are strongly recommended; pasteurized dairy is considered inferior and potentially inflammatory.

## Background

Dairy is one of the most debated foods in nutrition. Carnivore and keto communities often exclude it; mainstream health advice promotes low-fat pasteurized versions. Saladino's position is different from both camps: he considers raw dairy one of the most nutrient-dense foods available, and he attributes the problems people associate with dairy almost entirely to the pasteurization process — not dairy itself.

## What Paul Saladino Recommends

### The Hierarchy

1. **Raw dairy** (first choice — always)
2. **Low-temperature pasteurized** (145°F for 20–40 min) — acceptable if raw is unavailable
3. **A2 pasteurized** — "decent," easier to digest than conventional A1, but still inferior to raw
4. **Conventional pasteurized A1 dairy** — last resort; he personally avoids it

### What Pasteurization Destroys

Standard pasteurization heats milk to 200–300°F (ultra-high temperature). Saladino says this kills:

- **Living lactase-producing bacteria** — the microbes that help digest lactose; their absence is why people become "lactose intolerant" to conventional milk
- **Active digestive enzymes** — including lactase itself
- **Immunoglobulins** — immune-active proteins with protective effects
- **Bioavailable vitamins and minerals** — reduced or structurally altered by heat
- **Whey protein conformation** — heat above 165°F denatures whey proteins, stripping their immune benefits and potentially triggering autoimmune and inflammatory reactions (skin issues, eczema, asthma)

He cites research that children who drink raw milk have lower rates of allergy, asthma, and hay fever — and that this protective effect does not exist with pasteurized milk.

### What He Personally Eats

- **Raw milk kefir** (~10 oz at breakfast) — he makes this himself by fermenting raw cow's milk with kefir grains; calls it a "super-probiotic"
- **Raw sour cream** — at breakfast
- **Raw butter** (~1 tablespoon at dinner)
- **Raw cheese** — a few ounces per day; his favorite is **Parmigiano Reggiano**
- **Heart & Soil Colostrum** supplement — in addition to food-based dairy
- Total: roughly **1 liter of raw milk equivalent** spread across meals per day

He notes he is personally lactose intolerant but has zero issues with raw kefir and raw cheese.

### Types of Dairy and What to Buy

**Milk:** Raw, grass-fed cow's or goat's milk. His partner brand is **RAW Farm USA** — the largest raw dairy farm in the US; they produce raw milk, raw cream, raw kefir, and raw butter. To find local sources, he recommends the **Raw Milk Finder at realmilk.com**.

**Butter:** Raw butter is "far superior" to pasteurized. Look for a bright yellow color — that indicates high beta-carotene from grass feeding. Raw butter from RAW Farm USA is his specific recommendation. Grass-fed pasteurized butter is acceptable if raw isn't available.

**Cheese:** Raw cheese is preferred. His specific favorites:
- **Parmigiano Reggiano** ("my favorite — don't be fooled by fake cheese")
- Raw cheese options available at **Trader Joe's** and **Whole Foods**

**Kefir:** Raw milk kefir made with kefir grains — either homemade or from RAW Farm's commercial kefir. He collaborated with RAW Farm and Erewhon on a raw kefir smoothie.

**Heavy cream and sour cream:** Both recommended in raw form. He lists raw heavy cream as one of his top grocery picks.

**Avoid entirely:** American cheese — the FDA doesn't classify it as real cheese (less than 50% actual cheese content); Saladino also notes it contains microplastics.

### A1 vs. A2 Milk

Most US dairy comes from Holstein cows producing A1 beta-casein, which is harder to digest. A2 beta-casein (found in some cow breeds, plus goats, sheep, and buffalo) is easier to digest. However, Saladino's view is that A1 vs. A2 is mainly relevant for people drinking processed pasteurized milk — raw milk is easy to digest regardless of beta-casein type because its enzymes and bacteria are intact.

### Lactose Intolerance

Saladino argues most lactose intolerance is a consequence of drinking pasteurized milk, not a fixed biological condition. Pasteurized milk lacks the lactase-producing bacteria and enzymes needed to break down lactose. Raw milk contains both, which is why many self-described lactose intolerant people tolerate it fine. For those still sensitive to raw milk, raw cheese and fermented raw kefir are the best options — fermentation eliminates most lactose.

## Key Points

- Raw dairy is one of the four pillars of the animal-based diet (alongside meat, organs, fruit, and honey)
- Pasteurization at 200–300°F kills enzymes, bacteria, and immune proteins — Saladino calls the result "dead milk"
- Raw milk from grass-fed animals retains full nutritional value and is tolerated by most lactose-intolerant people
- His daily dairy: raw kefir, raw sour cream, raw butter, and raw cheese (especially Parmigiano Reggiano), ~1 liter/day equivalent
- Preferred brand: **RAW Farm USA** for milk, cream, kefir, and butter
- Raw cheese is widely available at Trader Joe's and Whole Foods; use realmilk.com to find raw milk locally
- Low-temperature pasteurized (145°F) is acceptable when raw isn't available
- A2 pasteurized is better than A1 pasteurized, but still inferior to raw
- American cheese should be avoided entirely — it's a processed cheese product, not real cheese
- Children who consume raw dairy have lower rates of allergy and asthma per research Saladino cites

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking any dairy is fine** — the type matters enormously; pasteurized dairy is inferior and can trigger inflammation; raw is the goal
- **Dismissing dairy entirely due to lactose intolerance** — most intolerance is to pasteurized milk specifically; raw kefir and raw cheese are well-tolerated even by those who react to conventional milk
- **Buying A2 pasteurized and thinking it's equivalent to raw** — it's better than A1 but still pasteurized; the A1/A2 distinction matters mainly when the milk is processed
- **Using store-bought pasteurized yogurt as a probiotic** — raw kefir has a far richer probiotic profile; pasteurized yogurt contains far fewer live cultures and no active enzymes
- **Choosing pale yellow or white butter** — the color indicates grass-feeding; bright yellow butter = higher beta-carotene = more grass-fed
- **Eating American cheese or processed cheese products** — these are not real cheese; avoid entirely
- **Not knowing raw dairy is legal** — raw milk laws vary by state but raw cheese (aged 60+ days) is legal nationwide; it's more accessible than most people think

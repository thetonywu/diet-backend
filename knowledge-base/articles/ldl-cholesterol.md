# Will This Diet Raise My Cholesterol? Is High LDL Dangerous?

**Categories:** LDL / Cholesterol, Heart Disease

## TLDR

Yes, the animal-based diet can raise LDL — sometimes dramatically — but Paul Saladino argues this is not dangerous in metabolically healthy individuals. He considers the TG/HDL ratio, fasting insulin, and a coronary artery calcium (CAC) scan far more meaningful than LDL alone.

## Background

High LDL is one of the most common concerns people raise when considering a meat-heavy diet. Conventional medicine treats elevated LDL as a primary cardiovascular risk factor requiring treatment, often with statins. Saladino directly challenges this framing, arguing that LDL is a flawed marker that only poses risk in the context of metabolic dysfunction — and that most people eating animal-based are in fact becoming more metabolically healthy.

## What Paul Saladino Recommends

### His core position
Saladino argues that **LDL alone is not a reliable predictor of heart disease risk in metabolically healthy people**. He frequently cites his own numbers: LDL of 533 mg/dL and a coronary artery calcium (CAC) score of 0 — meaning no measurable plaque despite extremely elevated cholesterol.

His analogy: "LDL is the wood. Metabolic dysfunction is the spark. Without the spark, the wood doesn't catch fire."

### The markers he actually tracks
Rather than LDL, Saladino focuses on:

- **Fasting insulin** — target < 5 uIU/mL (his own: < 3 uIU/mL); the most important metabolic health marker
- **Triglycerides** — target ≤ 70 mg/dL
- **HDL** — target ≥ 60 mg/dL
- **TG/HDL ratio** — target < 2 (optimal is ~0.5); his primary cardiovascular risk marker
- **hsCRP** — a marker of systemic inflammation
- **Fasting glucose** — target 70–85 mg/dL; HbA1c ≤ 5.3%
- **CAC score** — imaging of actual coronary plaque; he considers this the gold standard; his own score is 0

He downweights LDL-C and total cholesterol when these metabolic markers are favorable.

### If your LDL rises on this diet
Saladino's framework for someone whose doctor flags high LDL:

1. **Check metabolic health first.** If fasting insulin is < 5, TG < 70, HDL > 60, and TG/HDL < 2, he considers the elevated LDL clinically unimportant.
2. **Get a CAC scan.** This directly measures plaque burden and is more informative than lipid numbers alone. If CAC is 0, there is no established plaque risk.
3. **Do not take statins** unless imaging confirms actual plaque progression. Saladino warns statins increase risk of diabetes, insulin resistance, and cognitive decline.

### The lean mass hyper-responder (LMHR) phenomenon
Many lean, athletic people on low-carb or animal-based diets experience dramatic LDL increases — often to 200–400+ mg/dL — while simultaneously showing excellent HDL and very low triglycerides. Researcher Dave Feldman (cholesterolcode.com) named this the **Lean Mass Hyper-Responder (LMHR)** phenotype and proposed the "Lipid Energy Model" to explain it: in lean, insulin-sensitive individuals, low carb intake drives increased VLDL secretion and LDL turnover as an energy transport mechanism.

A 2024 study (the **KETO trial**, published in JACC Advances) followed 100 LMHR individuals on ketogenic diets — average LDL of 272 mg/dL — against matched controls with LDL of 123 mg/dL. CT angiography found **no significant difference in coronary plaque** between groups. Saladino cites this as direct evidence that high LDL in the LMHR context does not cause atherosclerosis.

### His critique of the diet-heart hypothesis
- **Ancel Keys' data was cherry-picked**: the Seven Countries Study excluded countries (like France) that contradicted the saturated fat → heart disease theory; Greek data was collected during Lent, skewing results
- **Oxidized LDL, not LDL itself, is the atherogenic form**: seed oils high in omega-6 linoleic acid oxidize LDL; saturated animal fat does not
- **Longevity data cuts the other way**: a Swedish 35-year study found higher cholesterol correlated with reaching age 100 (PMID: 37726432); Chinese centenarian data showed better survival with cholesterol > 131 mg/dL (PMID: 35646784)
- **Minnesota Coronary Experiment**: when saturated fat was replaced with vegetable oils and LDL dropped ~14%, all-cause mortality *increased* with each 30-point drop in cholesterol

## Key Points

- The animal-based diet commonly raises LDL, sometimes to 200–500+ mg/dL, particularly in lean, active individuals (the LMHR phenotype)
- Saladino argues elevated LDL is not dangerous when fasting insulin is low, triglycerides are low (≤70), and HDL is high (≥60)
- The TG/HDL ratio is his preferred cardiovascular risk marker — target below 2, optimal around 0.5
- A CAC (coronary artery calcium) score of 0 indicates no measurable plaque, regardless of LDL level
- The 2024 KETO trial found no difference in coronary plaque between LMHR individuals with LDL averaging 272 mg/dL and matched controls at 123 mg/dL
- Saladino attributes atherosclerosis primarily to insulin resistance and seed oil-driven LDL oxidation, not dietary saturated fat or LDL elevation itself
- He cautions against statins, citing associated risks of diabetes, insulin resistance, and cognitive decline
- High cholesterol in elderly populations is associated with longer life in multiple studies he cites

## Common Mistakes

- **Panicking over an LDL number without context** — LDL in isolation tells you very little; the metabolic panel (fasting insulin, TG, HDL) and ideally a CAC scan are needed to interpret it
- **Starting statins without imaging** — Saladino argues statins are rarely warranted for metabolically healthy people with high LDL and no plaque
- **Conflating all LDL** — a diet producing large, buoyant LDL particles alongside very low TG and high HDL is very different from the small, dense LDL pattern associated with metabolic syndrome
- **Ignoring fasting insulin** — this is the marker Saladino considers most important; conventional labs often don't include it unless requested

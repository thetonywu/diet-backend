# What Are Some Easy Recipes and How Do I Meal Prep?

**Categories:** Recipes & Meal Ideas, Animal-Based Diet Basics

## TLDR

Animal-based cooking is intentionally simple — most meals require minimal preparation. Paul Saladino's staple meals are ground beef burgers, scrambled eggs with raw yolks, pan-seared steak, and raw or lightly seared liver, paired with seasonal fruit and raw honey. Meal prep centers on batch-cooking ground beef, pre-portioning organ meats, and keeping fruit and raw dairy stocked. No complex recipes are needed.

## Background

People coming from the standard American diet often overthink animal-based cooking. The diet's simplicity is a feature, not a limitation — meals are built around high-quality animal protein, cooking fat, salt, and fruit. Saladino's personal eating pattern demonstrates that three to four ingredients per meal is typical. The learning curve is less about recipes and more about sourcing quality ingredients and mastering a few basic cooking techniques.

## What Paul Saladino Recommends

**His core cooking techniques (these cover 90% of meals):**

- **Ground beef burgers:** Form 1/3 lb patties from grass-fed ground beef, season with Redmond Sea Salt, grill or pan-sear in tallow or ghee. This is his most frequent meal.
- **Scrambled eggs:** Cook the whites slowly in butter or ghee over low heat to neutralize avidin (a protein in raw whites that blocks biotin absorption). Once whites are set, fold in raw yolks at the end to preserve heat-sensitive nutrients. Never fully cook the yolks.
- **Steak:** Pan-sear or grill grass-fed ribeye, strip, or more affordable cuts (skirt, bavette, chuck) in tallow. Season with Redmond Sea Salt only. Cook to rare or medium-rare.
- **Liver:** Freeze for 2+ weeks (kills potential parasites), then slice thin and eat raw, or lightly sear in butter for 30 seconds per side. Target 0.5–1 oz per day. Freezing also makes the flavor milder.
- **Bone broth:** Simmer grass-fed beef bones (knuckle, marrow, oxtail) for 12–24 hours with a splash of apple cider vinegar to extract minerals. Rich in glycine and collagen for gut health, sleep, and connective tissue.

**Beginner-friendly daily template:**

| Meal | What to make | Time |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-workout | Raw milk + raw honey + creatine + sea salt | 2 min |
| Breakfast | 4 scrambled eggs (whites cooked, raw yolks folded in) + fruit | 10 min |
| Lunch | 1/2 lb ground beef burger + fruit or honey | 10 min |
| Dinner | Steak or ground beef + 0.5 oz liver + fruit | 15 min |

**Meal prep strategies:**

- **Batch cook ground beef:** Brown 3–5 lbs of grass-fed ground beef at once in tallow. Store in glass containers in the fridge for 3–4 days. Reheat portions as needed.
- **Pre-portion liver:** Slice fresh liver into 0.5 oz pieces, freeze individually on a sheet pan, then transfer to a glass container. Pull one piece daily.
- **Stock fruit:** Keep a rotating supply of ripe bananas, mangoes, berries, and watermelon. Cut watermelon into cubes for easy snacking or juice it fresh.
- **Make bone broth weekly:** One large pot of bone broth lasts the week. Store in glass mason jars in the fridge or freeze extras.
- **Pre-make tallow:** Render beef suet into tallow in a slow cooker (low heat for 8–12 hours, strain through cheesecloth). Store in glass jars at room temperature. One batch lasts weeks.
- **Keep raw dairy and honey stocked:** Raw milk, raw honey, and Redmond Sea Salt are grab-and-go staples that require zero prep.

**Carb sides (no cooking required):**
- Sliced mango, papaya, or watermelon
- Whole bananas or oranges
- Fresh blueberries
- Raw honey drizzled over fruit or eaten by the spoonful
- Fresh-squeezed orange juice
- Coconut water

## Key Points

- Animal-based cooking is deliberately minimalist. Most meals are one protein + one fat + salt + fruit.
- Ground beef is the most practical everyday staple — affordable, fast to cook, and easy to batch prep.
- Eggs should have whites fully cooked (to neutralize avidin) but yolks left raw or runny to preserve nutrients.
- Liver is most palatable when frozen for 2+ weeks, sliced thin, and eaten raw or barely seared. Small daily portions (0.5 oz) are easier to sustain than large weekly servings.
- Bone broth is the only "recipe" that takes significant time, but it's passive cooking (simmer and forget).
- All cooking should use tallow, butter, or ghee — never seed oils.
- Season with Redmond Sea Salt only. No need for complex spice blends.
- Meal prep on this diet is less about elaborate Sunday cooking sessions and more about keeping quality staples stocked and a few proteins batch-cooked.

## Common Mistakes

- **Overcomplicating meals.** The diet works because it's simple. You don't need recipes with 10+ ingredients. Salt, heat, and quality meat are sufficient.
- **Fully cooking egg yolks.** Heat destroys cholesterol and fat-soluble vitamins in yolks. Cook the whites, fold in raw yolks at the end.
- **Eating liver in large weekly doses instead of small daily portions.** A 0.5 oz daily slice is sustainable and avoids excess vitamin A. Large portions are unpleasant and unnecessary.
- **Storing food in plastic containers.** Always use glass for meal prep storage. Plastic leaches endocrine disruptors, especially when reheated.
- **Cooking with seed oils "just this once."** Even occasional seed oil exposure adds linoleic acid to tissues. Keep tallow, butter, and ghee as the only cooking fats.
- **Skipping bone broth.** It's the primary source of glycine, collagen, and connective tissue nutrients. A weekly batch takes minimal active effort.
- **Thinking you need a cookbook.** Paul Saladino's meal pattern is essentially the same 4–5 meals rotated. Master those and you're set.

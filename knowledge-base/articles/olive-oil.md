# Which Olive Oils Are Actually Real and Safe?

**Categories:** Seed Oils, Toxin Avoidance

## TLDR

Saladino considers olive oil a "medium toxicity" fat — not a seed oil, but not his first choice either. Animal fats (tallow, butter, ghee) are always preferred for cooking. Commercial olive oil is frequently adulterated with cheap seed oils, making most supermarket bottles unreliable. If you use olive oil, buy from reputable sources, but don't treat it as a health food or primary cooking fat.

## Background

Olive oil occupies a unique position in the diet debate. Mediterranean diet advocates consider it a cornerstone of heart health, while strict carnivore dieters avoid all plant oils. Saladino's position falls between these camps: olive oil is a fruit oil (not a seed oil), has a better fatty acid profile than industrial seed oils, but is still inferior to animal fats and carries a serious adulteration problem that makes most commercial products untrustworthy.

## What Paul Saladino Recommends

**His fat hierarchy (from the Heart & Soil food list):**

| Tier | Fats | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Low toxicity | Butter, tallow, ghee, suet, lard | Primary cooking fats — use freely |
| Medium toxicity | Olive oil, avocado oil, coconut oil, palm oil | Acceptable in moderation |
| High toxicity | Canola, corn, soybean, sunflower, safflower, cottonseed, peanut, grapeseed, rice bran | Eliminate completely |

**On olive oil specifically:**
- It is a fruit oil, not a seed oil — higher in monounsaturated fat (oleic acid) and lower in inflammatory linoleic acid than the "hateful eight" seed oils
- He does not demonize it the way he does seed oils, but he also does not actively recommend it
- He discourages "excessive use of even 'healthy' plant oils" — tallow, butter, and ghee are always preferred
- Olive oil is never listed among his approved cooking fats in any dietary guide

**The adulteration problem:**
- Commercial olive oil is "frequently adulterated with seed oils" — cheaper canola or soybean oil is blended in
- Restaurant "olive oil" is often an "olive oil blend" that is mostly canola
- Avocado oil has the same problem — studies have found most commercial avocado oil fails purity tests
- This adulteration means that buying cheap supermarket olive oil may expose you to the very seed oils you're trying to avoid

**If you choose to use olive oil:**
- Buy from reputable, single-origin sources
- Look for a harvest date on the bottle (not just a "best by" date)
- Choose dark glass bottles (light degrades olive oil)
- Extra virgin cold-pressed is the only grade worth buying — refined olive oil has been chemically processed
- Use it raw (salad dressings, finishing) rather than for high-heat cooking — olive oil has a lower smoke point than animal fats

**One positive mention:** Saladino recommends vitamin D3 supplements in an olive oil carrier for people with limited sun exposure, suggesting he considers olive oil safe as a supplement base.

## Key Points

- Olive oil is classified as "medium toxicity" on the Heart & Soil food list — acceptable but not optimal
- It is a fruit oil (pressed from olives), not a seed oil, and has a fundamentally different fatty acid profile
- Animal fats (tallow, butter, ghee) are always preferred as primary cooking and eating fats
- Most commercial olive oil is adulterated with cheaper seed oils, making it an unreliable product
- Commercial avocado oil has the same adulteration problem
- At restaurants, "cooked in olive oil" claims are often false — many kitchens cut olive oil with canola or use blended oils
- If buying olive oil, prioritize single-origin, extra virgin, cold-pressed, with a harvest date and dark glass packaging
- Saladino does not cook with olive oil — his approved cooking fats are tallow, butter, ghee, lard, and coconut oil

## Common Mistakes

- **Treating olive oil as a health food** — while better than seed oils, it's still a plant fat that Saladino ranks below animal fats; don't use it as your primary cooking fat
- **Buying cheap supermarket olive oil and assuming it's pure** — adulteration is widespread; cheap olive oil is often mostly canola or soybean oil
- **Cooking at high heat with olive oil** — it has a lower smoke point than tallow or ghee; use animal fats for searing, frying, and roasting
- **Trusting restaurant claims of "cooked in olive oil"** — most restaurants use seed oils or blends regardless of what they say
- **Confusing "light olive oil" with healthy** — "light" refers to flavor, not calories; it's typically refined and chemically processed
- **Assuming avocado oil is safer** — commercial avocado oil has even higher adulteration rates than olive oil in some studies

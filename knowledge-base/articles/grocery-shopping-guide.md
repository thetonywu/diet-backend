# Where Should I Shop for the Animal-Based Diet?

**Categories:** Food Hauls & Shopping, Budget & Practical Tips

## TLDR

Paul Saladino recommends sourcing grass-fed meat from regenerative farms online (White Oak Pastures, Force of Nature), buying raw dairy through RealMilk.com, and supplementing with local butchers and farmers markets for organ meats. Costco and Whole Foods can work for staples like grass-fed ground beef and seasonal fruit, but most conventional grocery stores carry too many seed-oil-laden products to be reliable primary sources.

## Background

One of the first practical questions people face when starting the animal-based diet is where to actually buy the food. Grass-fed ruminant meat, organ meats, raw dairy, and quality fruit aren't always easy to find at a standard supermarket. Saladino emphasizes that sourcing quality ingredients is just as important as knowing what to eat — conventional meat from grain-fed animals has a fundamentally different fatty acid profile than grass-finished alternatives.

## What Paul Saladino Recommends

Saladino's shopping strategy prioritizes quality sourcing over convenience. He recommends building a supply chain from multiple sources rather than relying on a single store.

**Online regenerative farms (his top recommendation):**
- White Oak Pastures (whiteoakpastures.com) — regenerative farm in Georgia offering grass-fed beef shanks, ground beef, suet, liver, and other organs at competitive prices.
- Force of Nature Meats (forceofnature.com) — Texas-based regenerative operation specializing in affordable ground meat, plus venison, elk, and bison.
- US Wellness Meats — another trusted source for grass-fed and grass-finished meats.
- Nourish Food Club and Nose To Tail — specifically recommended for low-PUFA (low polyunsaturated fat) meat selections.
- Eat Wild directory (eatwild.com) — locates local farms selling grass-fed meat near you.

**Local sourcing:**
- Farmers markets for fresh organ meats, pastured eggs, and seasonal fruit.
- Local butchers — organ meats (liver, heart, kidney) are often $5/lb or less because demand is low. Some butchers give them away.
- Whole Foods — carries grass-fed ground beef, some organ meats, and quality fruit. Useful for filling gaps.

**Raw dairy:**
- RealMilk.com — directory for locating raw milk suppliers in your area. Raw dairy is a cornerstone of Saladino's protocol.

**What to buy at conventional grocery stores (Costco, Trader Joe's, etc.):**
- Seasonal fruit: watermelon, blueberries, mango, papaya, oranges, bananas.
- Raw honey (check for glyphosate-free labels).
- Pasture-raised eggs.
- Kerrygold butter (Irish grass-fed) as a backup when raw butter isn't available.
- Redmond Sea Salt.
- Avoid browsing processed food aisles — nearly everything packaged contains seed oils.

**Dining out:**
- LocalFats.com — identifies seed-oil-free restaurants near you.
- BBQ joints, steakhouses, and sushi restaurants are generally the safest options.

## Key Points

- Online regenerative farms are the most reliable source for properly raised grass-finished meat with the correct fatty acid profile.
- Local butchers are the cheapest source for organ meats, which are the most nutrient-dense foods in the diet.
- Raw dairy requires finding a local supplier — RealMilk.com is the best starting directory.
- Conventional grocery stores work for fruit, honey, eggs, and butter, but are unreliable for quality meat.
- Avoid buying conventional chicken and pork at any store — grain-fed poultry and pork are high in linoleic acid regardless of the retailer.
- Buying in bulk (whole or half animals from local farms) and using subscribe-and-save programs from online farms significantly reduces per-pound costs.
- The Heart & Soil store locator (heartandsoil.co/store-locator/) helps find their organ supplements in retail locations.

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming Costco grass-fed is equivalent to regenerative farm quality.** Big-box "grass-fed" labels can be misleading — some cattle are grass-fed but grain-finished, which changes the fat composition.
- **Skipping organ meats because the grocery store doesn't carry them.** Local butchers, ethnic markets, and online farms almost always have them.
- **Buying conventional animal fat.** Lean conventional beef is an acceptable budget compromise, but conventional fat concentrates pesticides and omega-6. Always prioritize grass-fed fat sources.
- **Over-relying on one store.** The animal-based diet works best with a multi-source approach: online farms for meat, local suppliers for dairy, and grocery stores for fruit and pantry staples.
- **Not checking restaurant cooking oils.** Most restaurants cook with seed oils. Ask about cooking fats or stick to places listed on LocalFats.com.

# Are Seed Oils Really That Bad? What Does the Science Say?

**Categories:** Seed Oils

## TLDR

Paul Saladino considers seed oils to be among the most harmful components of the modern diet, primarily due to their high linoleic acid (omega-6 fat) content. He argues linoleic acid accumulates in body fat, drives inflammation, and is "the biggest driver of chronic disease." He recommends completely eliminating seed oils and replacing them with saturated fats from animal sources — tallow, butter, and ghee.

## Background

Seed oils — canola, soybean, corn, sunflower, safflower, cottonseed, and similar industrial vegetable oils — are a modern invention. Their widespread use in the food supply only began in the early 20th century, with soybean oil consumption alone increasing roughly 1,000-fold between 1909 and 1999. Before industrialization, humans consumed very little polyunsaturated fat from plant sources, making seed oils a significant and recent change to the human diet. Whether that change is harmful or neutral is one of the more contested debates in nutrition science.

## What Paul Saladino Recommends

Paul Saladino's position is unambiguous: seed oils are not a species-appropriate fat and should be eliminated entirely. His reasoning is built around linoleic acid, the primary omega-6 fatty acid in most seed oils.

**His core argument:**
- Modern Americans consume up to 20% of their calories from linoleic acid. Hunter-gatherer populations like the Hadza and Maasai consume only 2–3%.
- Excess linoleic acid accumulates in fat cells, causing them to enlarge and release free fatty acids, which induces insulin resistance in peripheral muscles.
- Linoleic acid is highly prone to oxidation. When oxidized — during cooking or industrial processing — it generates harmful byproducts including aldehydes and lipid peroxides that damage cells and mitochondria.
- He argues oxidized LDL (primed by linoleic acid) is the relevant cardiovascular risk factor, not total LDL.
- Eliminating seed oils is the "most important lever to pull" for improving health, more impactful than any other single dietary change.

**Actionable recommendations from Saladino:**
- Remove all seed oils from cooking: canola, soybean, corn, sunflower, safflower, cottonseed, grapeseed, and rice bran oil.
- Cook with tallow, butter, ghee, lard, or coconut oil instead.
- Avoid conventionally raised pork and poultry, which are fed corn and soy and therefore have high linoleic acid in their fat.
- Read labels on packaged foods — seed oils are hidden in bread, crackers, chips, granola, salad dressings, sauces, restaurant foods, and almost all processed items.
- Choose pasture-raised ruminant meats (beef, lamb, bison), which have a much more favorable fat profile.
- Saladino also discussed seed oil dangers extensively with researcher Tucker Goodrich on his *Fundamental Health* podcast (episode 123: "Ending the debate over seed oils") and with other linoleic acid researchers in a compiled ebook.

## Key Points

- **"The hateful eight" seed oils to avoid:** canola, corn, cottonseed, soybean, sunflower, safflower, grapeseed, and rice bran oil.
- Seed oils are industrial products made by crushing seeds, extracting oil with petroleum-based solvents like hexane, then bleaching and deodorizing at high heat — a process that generates oxidation byproducts before the oil even reaches store shelves.
- Linoleic acid is unstable. Unlike saturated fat, it has multiple double bonds that react readily with oxygen and heat, producing aldehydes and other toxic byproducts.
- Because linoleic acid incorporates into cell membranes and fat tissue, it takes months to years to fully clear from the body after eliminating dietary sources.
- Processed foods are the largest hidden source of seed oils — eliminating them requires avoiding nearly all packaged and restaurant food.
- Conventionally raised chicken and pork are secondary vectors, as they are fed high-linoleic-acid grain diets that concentrate in their fat.
- Saladino references the Minnesota Coronary Experiment (1960s) as historical evidence that replacing saturated fat with vegetable oil did not reduce heart disease deaths despite lowering LDL cholesterol.
- The mainstream position, supported by the American Heart Association and a 2020 Cochrane review of over 53,000 participants, holds that replacing saturated fats with unsaturated fats (including seed oils) reduces cardiovascular risk. A 2019 meta-analysis in *Circulation* found higher blood linoleic acid levels were associated with lower cardiovascular event risk.
- Saladino's view represents a minority position in institutional nutrition science but has significant support in the ancestral health and animal-based diet communities.

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming "vegetable oil" is plant-based and therefore healthy.** Most seed oils are industrially processed products that bear little resemblance to whole plant foods.
- **Thinking olive oil is in the same category.** Saladino distinguishes seed oils from olive oil and avocado oil, which are fruit oils with different fatty acid profiles (higher in monounsaturated fat, lower in linoleic acid). However, he still recommends animal fats as primary cooking fats and notes that many commercial olive oils are adulterated.
- **Only avoiding oil at home while ignoring restaurant food.** Virtually all restaurant cooking uses seed oils. This is one of the biggest sources of dietary linoleic acid for people trying to eliminate it.
- **Switching from beef to chicken thinking it's healthier.** Conventionally raised chicken is very high in linoleic acid and is one of the main drivers of excess omega-6 intake on a standard diet.
- **Expecting immediate results.** Because linoleic acid incorporates into body fat, it takes 2–4 years of low-linoleic-acid eating to fully replace it in tissues. Short-term elimination studies may not capture the full benefit.
- **Conflating all polyunsaturated fats.** Omega-3 fats (from fatty fish, grass-fed beef) are also polyunsaturated but are not the issue. The concern is specifically with omega-6 linoleic acid and the extreme imbalance in the modern omega-6 to omega-3 ratio.

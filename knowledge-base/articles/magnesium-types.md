# What Are the Different Types of Magnesium and Which Should I Take?

**Categories:** Supplements, Nutrients

## TLDR

Most people are magnesium-deficient because it's one of the hardest minerals to get from animal foods alone. Saladino recommends magnesium glycinate for general use (well-absorbed, calming, good for sleep) or magnesium threonate for cognitive benefits (it crosses the blood-brain barrier). Avoid magnesium oxide — it's poorly absorbed and causes GI distress. Aim for 200-400mg elemental magnesium per day.

## Background

Magnesium is involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions in the body, including energy production, muscle contraction, nerve signaling, and sleep regulation. Unlike most micronutrients on the animal-based diet, magnesium is genuinely hard to get from meat and organs alone — it's more concentrated in plant foods, but those come with anti-nutrients that can block absorption. Saladino considers magnesium one of the few minerals where supplementation is reasonable even on a well-constructed animal-based diet, and includes it in his daily electrolyte protocol alongside sodium and potassium.

## What Paul Saladino Recommends

### Why Most People Need It

Saladino includes magnesium in his morning electrolyte protocol: a large glass of water with ~2-3g sodium (Redmond Sea Salt), magnesium, and potassium on waking. He experienced sleep disturbances, heart palpitations, and muscle cramps during his strict carnivore phase (2018-2020) before reintroducing carbohydrates and optimizing electrolytes. He considers magnesium supplementation reasonable for anyone eating animal-based because animal foods alone don't reliably cover the need.

### Forms He Recommends

- **Magnesium glycinate** — his primary recommendation for most people. Glycinate is a chelated form bound to the amino acid glycine, which gives it high bioavailability and a calming effect. It's well-tolerated with minimal GI side effects. Best choice for sleep support, muscle relaxation, and general deficiency correction.
- **Magnesium L-threonate** — his recommendation for cognitive benefits. This is the only form shown to cross the blood-brain barrier effectively, making it useful for memory, focus, and neuroprotection. More expensive than glycinate. Best choice if your primary goal is brain function.

### Forms to Avoid

- **Magnesium oxide** — the most common and cheapest form found in drugstore supplements. Saladino specifically warns against it because it has very poor absorption (as low as 4%) and is the main offender for GI distress (diarrhea, cramping). Most of it passes through unabsorbed. It's essentially a laxative, not a magnesium supplement.

### Other Common Forms (Not Specifically Addressed by Saladino)

- **Magnesium citrate** — moderately well-absorbed but can cause loose stools at higher doses. Often used for constipation relief. Better than oxide but not his top pick.
- **Magnesium taurate** — bound to taurine, which has its own cardiovascular benefits. Sometimes recommended for heart health. Saladino hasn't publicly endorsed or criticized this form.
- **Magnesium malate** — bound to malic acid. Sometimes recommended for energy production and muscle pain (fibromyalgia). Saladino hasn't publicly addressed it.

### Dosing

- **200-400mg elemental magnesium per day** — this is the elemental amount, not the total weight of the supplement compound
- Taken in the morning as part of his electrolyte protocol with sodium and potassium
- He does not publicly name a preferred magnesium brand
- He does not sell a magnesium product through Heart & Soil or Lineage Provisions

## Key Points

- Magnesium is one of the few nutrients that's genuinely hard to get from animal foods alone, making supplementation reasonable even on a well-constructed animal-based diet
- Magnesium glycinate is the best all-around form: high absorption, minimal GI issues, calming effect that supports sleep
- Magnesium threonate is the best form for cognitive benefits because it crosses the blood-brain barrier
- Magnesium oxide is essentially worthless as a supplement — 4% absorption rate, mostly acts as a laxative
- Dose 200-400mg elemental magnesium per day; "elemental" means the actual magnesium content, not the total pill weight
- Magnesium deficiency can contribute to sleep problems, muscle cramps, heart palpitations, anxiety, and poor recovery
- Take it as part of a morning electrolyte protocol with sodium (Redmond Sea Salt) and potassium
- Don't confuse "milligrams of magnesium glycinate" with "milligrams of elemental magnesium" — the elemental content is what matters and is usually listed separately on the label

## Common Mistakes

- **Buying cheap magnesium oxide** — it's the default in most drugstore multivitamins and standalone supplements; check the label for the specific form
- **Not distinguishing elemental magnesium from compound weight** — a 500mg magnesium glycinate capsule contains far less than 500mg of actual magnesium; read the supplement facts panel for the elemental amount
- **Taking too much at once** — even well-absorbed forms can cause loose stools at high doses; split the dose if needed
- **Skipping magnesium because "I eat well"** — unlike most nutrients, magnesium is a genuine gap in animal-based diets; this is one supplement Saladino considers reasonable even for people eating organs
- **Not pairing with other electrolytes** — magnesium works alongside sodium and potassium; supplementing one without the others can create imbalances

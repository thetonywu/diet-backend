# Which Butter Should I Buy and Which Should I Avoid?

**Categories:** Animal-Based Diet Basics, Seed Oils

## TLDR

Paul Saladino recommends raw, grass-fed butter as the gold standard — his preferred brand is RAW Farm USA. Grass-fed pasteurized butter (like Kerrygold) is acceptable when raw isn't available. Conventional butter from grain-fed cows, margarine, and all butter substitutes should be avoided entirely. Ghee is treated as interchangeable with butter for cooking.

## Background

Butter has been demonized for decades by mainstream nutrition advice that blamed saturated fat for heart disease. Many people switched to margarine and seed-oil-based spreads, which Saladino considers far more harmful than the butter they replaced. For people transitioning to an animal-based diet, understanding which butter to buy is one of the most practical first steps — it's a daily-use fat that appears in cooking, baking, and eating.

## What Paul Saladino Recommends

Saladino classifies butter and ghee as "low toxicity" fats — his top tier alongside tallow, suet, and lard. Olive oil and avocado oil sit one tier below at "medium toxicity," and seed oils are "high toxicity" and should be eliminated completely.

**His butter hierarchy:**

1. **Raw, grass-fed butter** — always the first choice. His specific recommendation is **RAW Farm USA** raw butter. Raw butter retains all enzymes, beneficial bacteria, and fat-soluble vitamins destroyed by pasteurization.
2. **Grass-fed pasteurized butter** — acceptable when raw butter isn't available. Kerrygold is widely available and grass-fed, though Saladino hasn't explicitly endorsed it by name as a top pick.
3. **Conventional pasteurized butter** — last resort. Comes from grain-fed cows with a less favorable fatty acid profile (lower omega-3, lower K2, higher omega-6).

**How to spot quality butter:** Look for a bright yellow color, which indicates high beta-carotene content from grass feeding. Pale or white butter means the cows were grain-fed.

**Ghee:** Saladino lists ghee alongside butter and tallow as an approved cooking fat in every dietary guide. Ghee is clarified butter with milk solids removed, making it suitable for people who react to casein or lactose. He does not recommend specific ghee brands but uses it interchangeably with butter for high-heat cooking.

**What to avoid:**
- Margarine and all butter substitutes — these are made from seed oils (soybean, canola) and are among the worst fats you can eat
- "Light" or reduced-fat butter spreads — typically cut with water and seed oils
- Butter blends marketed as heart-healthy — usually mixed with canola or other vegetable oils

**His personal use:** Saladino eats approximately 1 tablespoon of raw butter at dinner daily, in addition to cooking with butter, ghee, and tallow throughout the day.

## Key Points

- Raw grass-fed butter is the best option; RAW Farm USA is his recommended brand
- Bright yellow color = grass-fed = higher beta-carotene, vitamin K2, CLA, and omega-3 content
- Grass-fed butter contains vitamin K2 (menaquinone), which directs calcium into bones and away from arteries — the Rotterdam Study found higher K2 intake significantly reduced coronary heart disease
- Grass-fed butter also contains conjugated linoleic acid (CLA), an anti-inflammatory fat, and a favorable omega-3 to omega-6 ratio
- Ghee is butter with milk solids removed — equally approved for cooking and suitable for those with dairy protein sensitivity
- Margarine is a seed oil product and should never be consumed
- At restaurants, Saladino's standard request is "Can you cook this in butter instead of vegetable oil?"
- Butter, tallow, and ghee are the three primary cooking fats on the animal-based diet — they replace all seed oils in the kitchen

## Common Mistakes

- **Choosing margarine or "plant butter" thinking it's healthier** — these are seed oil products disguised as butter alternatives; they contain the linoleic acid Saladino considers the biggest driver of chronic disease
- **Buying conventional butter without checking if it's grass-fed** — grain-fed butter has a worse fatty acid profile, lower vitamin K2, and less CLA
- **Ignoring butter color** — pale white butter almost always means grain-fed cows; bright yellow indicates grass feeding and higher nutrient density
- **Assuming all ghee is equal** — look for grass-fed ghee; conventional ghee from grain-fed cows has the same fatty acid problems as conventional butter
- **Thinking butter is bad for your heart** — Saladino argues saturated fat from grass-fed sources is protective, not harmful, and that the real cardiovascular risk comes from seed oils and oxidized linoleic acid
- **Not asking about cooking fat at restaurants** — most restaurants default to seed oils; requesting butter is one of the simplest harm-reduction strategies when eating out

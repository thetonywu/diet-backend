# How Much Sun Exposure Do I Need?

**Categories:** The Sun, Ancestral Lifestyle, Hormones

## TLDR

Paul Saladino recommends daily, direct sunlight exposure — especially morning sun — as a non-negotiable part of health optimization. He argues sunlight provides benefits that cannot be replicated by a vitamin D pill, including testosterone production via a skin-brain-gonad axis, nitric oxide synthesis, and circadian rhythm regulation. He opposes chemical sunscreens and recommends zinc oxide-based alternatives when needed.

## Background

Modern humans spend the vast majority of their time indoors under artificial lighting, a dramatic departure from the outdoor lifestyles of ancestral populations. Saladino argues this indoor shift is a significant contributor to declining testosterone levels, poor sleep, vitamin D deficiency, and disrupted circadian rhythms. He views daily sun exposure as a fundamental input the body requires to function optimally — on par with diet quality and sleep.

## What Paul Saladino Recommends

Saladino treats sunlight as a hormonal and metabolic intervention, not just a source of vitamin D.

**Daily sun protocol:**
- Get direct morning sunlight first thing after waking — he calls this "the most powerful circadian rhythm signal" for setting the internal clock and priming melatonin release in the evening
- Expose skin throughout the day, not just the face — the body synthesizes vitamin D and nitric oxide in the skin from UV exposure
- Use the free **D-Minder app** to calculate how much sun you need based on your location, skin type, and the UV index on a given day
- Avoid sunscreen during normal daily exposure — blocking UV blocks vitamin D synthesis and hormonal signaling
- He personally surfs daily and gets substantial unprotected sun exposure

**Sunscreen stance:**
- Chemical sunscreens are hormone disruptors — he specifically names oxybenzone, avobenzone, and octinoxate as chemicals to avoid
- If sun protection is needed (extended beach time, high UV), use a tallow-based sunscreen with zinc oxide (mineral, not chemical)
- He argues that eliminating seed oils from the diet reduces sunburn susceptibility, as linoleic acid in skin tissue increases photosensitivity and oxidative damage from UV

**Vitamin D supplementation:**
- Strong preference for sun over pills: "the benefits from sunlight exposure literally cannot be obtained from a vitamin D pill"
- Sunlight produces nitric oxide, regulates hormones, shifts the gut microbiome, and entrains circadian rhythm — none of which a D3 capsule provides
- For those with limited sun access (winter, indoor jobs), vitamin D3 in an olive oil carrier is an acceptable fallback — but not a replacement

## Key Points

- UVB light activates a skin-brain-gonad axis that increases testosterone and libido, per a 2021 Cell Reports study (PMID 34433056) that Saladino frequently cites
- Morning sunlight is the strongest signal for setting circadian rhythm, which governs melatonin production, sleep quality, and hormone release cycles
- Sunlight drives nitric oxide production in the skin, which lowers blood pressure and improves cardiovascular function
- UVB exposure has been shown to shift gut microbiome composition — a benefit not achievable through supplementation
- Chemical sunscreen ingredients (oxybenzone, avobenzone, octinoxate) are absorbed into the bloodstream and act as endocrine disruptors
- The testes contain vitamin D receptors that respond directly to sun exposure — Saladino notes that "even getting some sunlight on your genitals can have a variety of benefits"
- A diet high in seed oils may increase sunburn risk by loading skin cell membranes with oxidation-prone linoleic acid

## Common Mistakes

- **Avoiding sun and relying on vitamin D pills** — this captures only one of many benefits of sunlight; Saladino calls it "blocking vitamin D synthesis and the broader suite of sun benefits"
- **Using chemical sunscreen for all outdoor time** — routine use blocks UV-mediated hormone production and exposes you to endocrine-disrupting chemicals
- **Only getting sun on the face and hands** — larger skin surface area means more vitamin D and nitric oxide production; expose arms, legs, and torso when possible
- **Ignoring sun because of skin cancer fears** — Saladino argues that ancestral populations had extensive sun exposure without epidemic skin cancer rates, and that diet (particularly seed oil elimination) modulates UV damage
- **Not adjusting for season and latitude** — UV intensity varies dramatically; use D-Minder to avoid both under-exposure (winter) and over-exposure (midday summer)

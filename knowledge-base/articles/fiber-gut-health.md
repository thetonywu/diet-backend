# What About Fiber? How Does Gut Health Work Without Vegetables?

**Categories:** Gut Health, Vegetables

## TLDR

Paul Saladino argues that dietary fiber is unnecessary and, for many people, actively harmful. He contends that removing fiber resolves bloating, constipation, and IBS-type symptoms, and that the microbiome thrives on fermented animal foods and environmental exposure — not plant fiber. He cites specific studies showing zero-fiber diets outperform high-fiber diets for digestive health.

## Background

"But what about fiber?" is one of the first questions people ask when considering a diet built around animal foods. Mainstream nutrition treats fiber as essential for gut health, regularity, and colon cancer prevention. Saladino challenges each of these claims directly, calling fiber a "fairy tale" and arguing that plant-based fiber causes more gut problems than it solves — especially for people already dealing with bloating, IBS, or leaky gut.

## What Paul Saladino Recommends

### His core argument: fiber is not necessary and often harmful
Saladino's case against fiber rests on three pillars:

1. **The evidence for fiber is weak.** The modern fiber hypothesis traces to surgeon Denis Burkitt's 1970s observational work on African populations. Saladino argues Burkitt's data was badly confounded. Two colonoscopy-based studies he cites directly contradict it:
   - A 2019 *Journal of Gastroenterology* study (n=2,100): more fiber was associated with *more* diverticulosis, not less
   - A 2013 *Clinical Gastroenterology and Hepatology* study found no protective link between fiber and diverticulosis at all

2. **Fiber causes the constipation and bloating it's supposed to fix.** Undigested fiber reaches the colon and is fermented by bacteria, producing hydrogen, carbon dioxide, and methane — causing gas, bloating, and distension. Methane-producing bacteria slow gut motility, which is what causes constipation in the first place.

3. **Removing fiber resolves these symptoms.** Saladino's flagship citation is a 2012 study in the *World Journal of Gastroenterology* (Ho et al.) involving 63 constipated patients split into zero-fiber, reduced-fiber, and unchanged-diet groups. The zero-fiber group had **100% resolution** of constipation, gas, and bloating — every participant in that arm saw complete symptom resolution.

### What about the microbiome?
The standard concern is that without fiber, gut bacteria starve and diversity collapses. Saladino disputes this:

- **Alpha diversity doesn't require fiber.** He and collaborator Lucy Mailing, PhD (nutritional sciences, Univ. of Illinois) argue that adding fiber does not reliably increase microbiome diversity and removing it does not decrease it. Saladino reported high alpha diversity in his own microbiome testing after 1.5 years on a zero-fiber carnivore diet.
- **Fermented foods outperform fiber.** He cites a 2021 Stanford *Cell* study (Sonnenburg et al., n=36) that compared a high-fiber diet to a high-fermented-food diet over 10 weeks. The high-fiber group showed no increase in microbiome diversity and no reduction in 19 inflammatory proteins measured. The fermented-food group showed increased diversity *and* reduced inflammation.
- **The Hadza example reframed.** The Hadza hunter-gatherers of Tanzania are frequently cited as proof that high-fiber diets produce superior gut microbiomes. Saladino argues the Hadza's tubers are so fibrous they spit out the fiber after chewing — their actual fiber intake is modest. He attributes their microbiome diversity to environmental factors: soil contact, outdoor living, and sunlight exposure.
- **Butyrate from other sources.** Bacteria also ferment fat and protein to produce isobutyrate and other short-chain fatty acids that fuel colonocytes. Dietary butter and ghee provide butyrate directly. Ketones from fat metabolism also supply colonocyte energy, substituting for microbially-produced butyrate.

### What he recommends for gut health instead

- **Eliminate plant irritants.** Lectins, oxalates, saponins, gluten, and seed oils are his primary culprits for gut inflammation and leaky gut. Removing these is step one.
- **Eat fermented animal foods.** Raw kefir, raw milk, fermented dairy, and raw sour cream are his preferred routes to microbiome support.
- **Bone broth.** One cup daily for compromised guts — provides collagen, glycine, and glutamine to support the gut lining.
- **Colostrum.** Grass-fed bovine colostrum (1.5–3g) provides immunoglobulins and bioactive proteins that support gut barrier integrity. Heart & Soil makes a colostrum product he recommends.
- **Sunlight.** He cites an interventional study showing UVB exposure directly shifts the gut microbiome — environmental exposure, not dietary fiber, is a key driver of microbial diversity.
- **Bone-in meats.** Gelatin from connective tissue supports the gut lining.

### Leaky gut and IBS
Saladino argues that **plant toxins — not fiber deficiency — are the primary cause of intestinal permeability (leaky gut)**:
- Gluten triggers zonulin release, opening gut tight junctions; he argues this happens in everyone, not just celiacs
- Saponins (found in quinoa), capsaicin, and lectins all damage the gut lining in ways he documents with specific study citations
- LPS (bacterial endotoxin) enters circulation through these gaps, triggering systemic inflammation and autoimmunity

His approach to IBS: remove fiber, remove plant irritants, add fermented animal foods and bone broth. He reports most IBS-type symptoms resolve within days to weeks.

## Key Points

- Dietary fiber is not necessary for healthy digestion or bowel regularity — fiber fermentation is a primary *cause* of bloating, gas, and constipation, not a cure
- A 2012 RCT found 100% resolution of constipation in patients who eliminated fiber entirely
- Two large colonoscopy-based studies found high fiber intake correlated with *more* diverticulosis, not less
- Microbiome diversity does not require fiber; a 2021 Stanford study found fermented foods outperform high-fiber diets for microbiome diversity and inflammation reduction
- Gut health on an animal-based diet is supported by fermented dairy, bone broth, colostrum, and sunlight — not plant fiber
- Plant lectins, saponins, gluten, and seed oils are Saladino's primary culprits for leaky gut and gut inflammation
- Stool volume is smaller without fiber — he frames this positively, as it indicates the body is absorbing nutrients rather than passing plant waste
- Removing fiber typically produces rapid improvement in bloating, gas, IBS symptoms, and constipation

## Common Mistakes

- **Expecting constipation when going fiber-free** — the evidence Saladino cites suggests the opposite: constipation typically improves or resolves after eliminating fiber
- **Treating probiotic supplements as equivalent to fermented foods** — Saladino prefers whole fermented foods (kefir, raw milk) over probiotic capsules, which he considers less effective
- **Assuming the microbiome will collapse** — he reports high microbial diversity on zero-fiber diets; diversity is driven more by environmental exposure than by fiber intake
- **Adding bone broth but keeping plant irritants** — healing the gut requires removing the root causes (lectins, gluten, seed oils), not just adding gut-supportive foods on top
